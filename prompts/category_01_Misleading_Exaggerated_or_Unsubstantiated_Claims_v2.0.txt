==========================
SUB-BUCKET 1: Unsubstantiated or Inadequately Supported Statements of Fact
==========================
(Including factual, statistical, or comparative assertions without evidence)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements of fact, comparison, or superiority that lack adequate data, evidentiary support, verification, or methodological explanation at the point of claim.

--- Prompt Set A: Absolute or Superlative Factual Claims Without Support ---
Purpose: Detect factual assertions presented as true without citation, data, or source.

Detection Question:
Does the content assert objective dominance, ranking, or status (e.g., best, top, most trusted, market leader) as fact, without identifying a defined universe, timeframe, data source, or methodology?

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "The best firm for producing top results."
‚Ä¢ "A market leader in active management."
‚Ä¢ "The top-performing strategy in its category."
‚Ä¢ "The most trusted investment provider."

--- Prompt Set B: Performance or Outcome Claims Without Evidence ---
Purpose: Capture claims of realized or demonstrable results, not goals.

Detection Question:
Does the statement assert actual performance, effectiveness, or outcomes (past or present) that would reasonably require performance data, results, or analysis, but none is provided?

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Proven to outperform over time."
‚Ä¢ "Consistently delivers strong results."
‚Ä¢ "Demonstrated superior performance."
‚Ä¢ "Track record of excellence."

Out of scope: "Seeks to," "aims to," "designed to"
--- Prompt Set C: Comparative Claims Without Identified Comparator ---
Purpose: Flag comparisons that cannot be evaluated.

Detection Question:
Does the statement compare performance, quality, or effectiveness relative to others, without identifying who/what the comparator is, the comparison universe, or the basis of comparison.

Important Guardrail: If the comparator exists elsewhere and is clearly linked, do not flag.


Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Outperforms peers."
‚Ä¢ "Better risk-adjusted returns."
‚Ä¢ "More effective than traditional approaches."
‚Ä¢ "Superior to competing strategies."

--- Prompt Set D: Quantitative-Sounding Claims Without Metrics ---
Purpose: Capture measurement-implying language, not vague adjectives.

Detection Question:
Does the statement use language that implies measurement, optimization, or magnitude, where a reasonable reader would expect defined metrics or calculations, but none are provided?

Key Filter: If the phrase sounds measurable, sub-bucket 1 may apply. If it is purely qualitative without measurement implication, defer to sub-bucket 7 only if material.


Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Delivers meaningful alpha."
‚Ä¢ "Low volatility with strong upside."
‚Ä¢ "Optimized risk-adjusted returns."
‚Ä¢ "Significant downside protection."

--- Prompt Set E: Vague Proof or Evidence Signals ---
Purpose: Capture claims that gesture toward evidence without providing it.

Detection Question:
Does the statement reference research, data, studies, or analysis as proof, without identifying the source, scope, or nature of that support?


Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Research shows‚Ä¶"
‚Ä¢ "Data confirms‚Ä¶"
‚Ä¢ "Studies demonstrate‚Ä¶"
‚Ä¢ "Analysis indicates‚Ä¶"

VERIFICATION INSTRUCTION (NOISE-REDUCING VERSION)

Before classifying under sub-bucket 1, affirmatively check whether the claim is supported by:
‚Ä¢ Data, figures, or tables
‚Ä¢ Clearly identified sources
‚Ä¢ Defined rankings or universes
‚Ä¢ Methodology descriptions
‚Ä¢ Footnotes or endnotes that are:
‚Ä¢ proximate,
‚Ä¢ readable, and
‚Ä¢ substantively responsive to the claim

If support exists and is adequate ‚Üí DO NOT FLAG.

If support exists but does not actually substantiate the claim ‚Üí sub-bucket 1 still applies.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify under Sub-Bucket 1 where statements are:
‚Ä¢ Clearly framed as opinion, belief, or philosophy
‚Ä¢ Aspirational or goal-oriented ("seeks," "aims," "designed to")
‚Ä¢ General positioning language without implied measurement or comparison

Opinion becomes in-scope only when framed as fact or superiority.

Do NOT flag under Sub-Bucket 1:
‚Ä¢ "providing stability and leadership"
‚Ä¢ "deep, broad perspectives"
‚Ä¢ "disciplined investment approach"
‚Ä¢ "experienced management team"
Unless the statement is explicitly tied to:
(a) performance outcomes,
(b) superiority claims, or
(c) quantitative results.

Additional Guardrails:
Do not classify statements as unsubstantiated where claims, terms, or phrases are:
‚Ä¢ Clearly framed in the context of the objective of fund or investment strategy (e.g., "we seek to mitigate risk," "we seek to anticipate," "we look to")
‚Ä¢ Clearly framed in the context of describing the author's organizational scale, infrastructure, or internal capabilities (e.g., "our extensive size and scale benefits our clients," "considerable value")
‚Ä¢ Otherwise considered superlative but permissible when, in the surrounding text, the author is explaining robustness and/or sophistication of their firm's capabilities without asserting that these capabilities outperform peers, meet a defined external benchmark, or deliver guaranteed results (e.g., "our firm has implemented world-class risk management systems to add value to our investment process," "our world-class investment philosophy")
‚Ä¢ Clearly framed as qualitative descriptor of process or explanatory descriptor, not as a quantified or outcome-based claim or guaranteed client benefit (e.g., "add considerable value," "add significant value")

Note: Superlatives like "world-class" are permissible in context when supported by surrounding explanation. However, if stated as a standalone material fact without backup, flag as unsubstantiated.


Boundary Reminder:
If the issue is lack of substantiation, classify here.
If the issue is certainty, exaggeration, or misleading framing, classify under Sub-Bucket 2 or 3.

==========================
SUB-BUCKET 2: Promissory or Certain-Outcome Language
==========================
(Explicit or functionally explicit guarantees of future results)

Sub-bucket 2 captures statements that present future investment outcomes as certain, inevitable, or assured ‚Äî rather than conditional, probabilistic, or subject to risk ‚Äî regardless of disclosures elsewhere.


Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Primary Test:
Identify statements that affirmatively convert future outcomes into certainty, by using language, structure, or framing that eliminates or materially neutralizes uncertainty, such that a reasonable investor would understand the result as assured rather than possible.

--- Prompt Set A: Explicit Certainty Verbs ---
Purpose: Detect language that asserts inevitability or assurance, even without the word "guarantee."

Detection Question:
Does the content use words such as will, ensures, delivers, guarantees, secures, cannot fail, eliminates risk, or similar constructions to describe outcomes? Consider whether the statement presents outcomes as inevitable rather than dependent on market conditions, assumptions, or uncertainty.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "This strategy will outperform over a full market cycle."
‚Ä¢ "Our disciplined process ensures superior long-term results."
‚Ä¢ "The portfolio is designed to deliver consistent outperformance."
‚Ä¢ "Investors cannot lose money with this approach."

--- Prompt Set B: Framing That Removes Uncertainty ---
Purpose: Capture language that frames risk or variability as resolved, even subtly.

Detection Question:
Does the statement frame future performance as predictable, inevitable, or effectively risk-free, rather than contingent on market conditions? Consider whether uncertainty is materially minimized or presented as eliminated.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "This approach removes the uncertainty of market timing."
‚Ä¢ "Our process takes the guesswork out of investing."
‚Ä¢ "Returns are engineered to be reliable across environments."
‚Ä¢ "Designed to perform regardless of market conditions."

--- Prompt Set C: Certainty via Institutional Authority ---
Purpose: Identify implied guarantees conveyed through authority, experience, or process.

Detection Question:
Does the content rely on institutional credibility, expertise, or process to imply certainty of outcome rather than probability? Consider whether authority is used to convert judgment into assurance.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Backed by decades of experience, the strategy will deliver results."
‚Ä¢ "Our proven framework ensures consistent success."
‚Ä¢ "Institutional discipline guarantees better outcomes for clients."

--- Prompt Set D: Implied Guarantees Without Absolute Words ---
Purpose: Detect certainty implied through tone, structure, or comparative framing.

Detection Question:
Does the statement imply a guaranteed or inevitable outcome even if no explicit promise is stated? Consider whether success is portrayed as reliable, dependable, or assured.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "A reliable way to generate superior returns."
‚Ä¢ "Built to consistently win in volatile markets."
‚Ä¢ "The smarter path to better performance."
‚Ä¢ "A dependable source of long-term growth."

--- Prompt Set E: Certainty Masked as Design Intent ---
Purpose: Prevent intent language from implying guaranteed results.

Detection Question:
Does the content describe goals, design, or intent in a way that implies the outcome will in fact occur? Consider whether intent language is used as a proxy for certainty.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "The strategy is designed to outperform benchmarks."
‚Ä¢ "Constructed to provide downside protection and upside capture."
‚Ä¢ "Engineered for superior risk-adjusted returns."

(Still promissory if not clearly qualified or supported.)

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify under Sub-Bucket 2 where statements are:
‚Ä¢ Clearly aspirational or goal-oriented
‚Ä¢ Expressly conditional or probabilistic
‚Ä¢ Limited to process description without outcome certainty
‚Ä¢ Forward-looking but appropriately hedged

Permissible examples (NOT Sub-Bucket 2):
‚Ä¢ "Seeks to outperform"
‚Ä¢ "Aims to generate long-term growth"
‚Ä¢ "May help reduce volatility"
‚Ä¢ "Intended to provide diversification benefits"

Do NOT flag under Sub-Bucket 2:
‚Ä¢ "Designed to seek long-term growth"
‚Ä¢ "A disciplined investment process"
‚Ä¢ "Focused on managing risk"
‚Ä¢ "Built with risk management in mind"
Unless the language removes uncertainty or presents outcomes as inevitable.


Aspirational language is permissible where uncertainty, assumptions, or risk are clearly conveyed and outcomes are not presented as assured.

Verification Instruction:
Before classifying under Sub-Bucket 2, confirm whether the same sentence contains meaningful hedging such as:
‚Ä¢ "may," "might," "could," "potentially"
‚Ä¢ "subject to risk," "depending on market conditions"
‚Ä¢ "no guarantee," "there is no assurance"

If hedging is present and not contradicted ‚Üí DO NOT FLAG under Sub-Bucket 2.

Hedging elsewhere does not cure Sub-Bucket 2 if the claim itself conveys certainty.

Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If future outcomes are presented as certain, assured, or inevitable ‚Äî rather than conditional or probabilistic ‚Äî classify under Sub-Bucket 2, even if accompanied by disclosures. Disclosures do not neutralize promissory language where the statement itself conveys certainty or inevitability.

==========================
SUB-BUCKET 3: Implied Guarantees or Certainty
==========================
(Created through framing, tone, or contextual emphasis‚Äîeven where no explicit guarantee is stated)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements that, through framing, tone, repetition, visual emphasis, or contextual placement, imply certainty of outcome or eliminate perceived risk ‚Äî even where no explicit guarantee or promissory language is used.

--- Prompt Set A: Certainty Created by Repetition or Emphasis ---
Purpose: Detect certainty implied through repeated messaging or dominant emphasis.

Detection Question:
Does the content repeatedly emphasize success, strength, or positive outcomes in a way that conveys inevitability rather than probability? Consider whether repetition crowds out uncertainty or risk.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Multiple references to "consistent success" without counterbalancing risk
‚Ä¢ Repeated use of "proven," "reliable," or "disciplined" without qualification
‚Ä¢ Headline and sub-headline both emphasizing superior outcomes

--- Prompt Set B: Certainty Through Selective Context or Omission ---
Purpose: Capture certainty implied by what is omitted or de-emphasized.

Detection Question:
Does the content present benefits, strengths, or outcomes without corresponding discussion of limitations, risks, or variability where balance is necessary to avoid a misleading impression? Consider whether omission creates a false sense of certainty.

Examples to flag:

Highlighting returns or success stories without mentioning variability
‚Ä¢ Describing downside protection without acknowledging limits
‚Ä¢ Presenting upside scenarios with no contextual risk framing

--- Prompt Set C: Tone That Conveys Assurance or Inevitability ---
Purpose: Identify certainty conveyed through confident, declarative, or absolute tone.

Detection Question:
Does the tone of the statement convey assurance, inevitability, or confidence beyond what the underlying facts or support reasonably justify? Consider whether tone substitutes for evidence.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "A strategy investors can rely on."
‚Ä¢ "Built for confidence in any market."
‚Ä¢ "A solution you can trust in uncertain times."

--- Prompt Set D: Certainty Through Visual or Structural Framing ---
Purpose: Detect certainty implied by layout, formatting, or prominence.

Detection Question:
Does the visual presentation or structural placement of statements emphasize certainty or success in a way that could override or diminish contextual caveats? Consider font size, headlines, call-outs, charts, or testimonials.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Bold or headline claims of success with buried qualifiers
‚Ä¢ Charts showing only favorable periods without explanation
‚Ä¢ Call-out boxes emphasizing outcomes without assumptions

--- Prompt Set E: Certainty Created by Narrative or Storytelling ---
Purpose: Capture certainty implied through narrative flow or storytelling.

Detection Question:
Does the narrative structure suggest a predictable or inevitable outcome based on selective examples or success stories? Consider whether anecdotes are presented as representative rather than illustrative.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Client success stories presented without variability context
‚Ä¢ Case studies implying repeatable outcomes without limits
‚Ä¢ Narratives that move directly from process ‚Üí success without uncertainty

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, evaluate the full context, including:
‚Ä¢ Surrounding language
‚Ä¢ Visual presentation
‚Ä¢ Disclosures and qualifiers
‚Ä¢ Balance between benefits and risks
If the overall presentation does not reasonably imply certainty, do not classify under Sub-Bucket 3.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify content solely because it:
‚Ä¢ Uses confident or professional tone, or
‚Ä¢ Highlights strengths or philosophy when appropriately balanced, contextualized, and not presented as inevitable outcomes
Confidence alone is not certainty.

Superlative words can be okay when:
‚Ä¢ They are used to describe how something is designed or intended to work, not to claim it is better than others.
‚Ä¢ They are descriptive, not comparative ‚Äî meaning they do not:
  ‚Ä¢ rank against peers,
  ‚Ä¢ compare to competitors, or
  ‚Ä¢ promise specific or guaranteed results.
When superlative language is used this way, it may be treated as descriptive context, not as a misleading claim.


Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If certainty or guaranteed outcomes are implied through framing, tone, emphasis, or omission, such that a reasonable investor could infer inevitability or risk-free results, classify under Sub-Bucket 3, even absent explicit promissory language. Disclosures that are technically present but overwhelmed by framing or emphasis do not cure the misleading impression.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):
‚Ä¢ Explicit promises ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 2
‚Ä¢ Lack of evidence ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 1
‚Ä¢ Implied certainty through presentation ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 3

==========================
SUB-BUCKET 4: Overstated, Absolute, or "Best-in-Class" Claims
==========================
(Lacking appropriate qualification, e.g., "leading," "unmatched," "the best")

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements that use absolute, superlative, or "best-in-class" type language to assert superiority, quality, or outcomes without appropriate qualification, definition, comparison basis, or evidentiary support.

--- Prompt Set A: Absolute or Superlative Language ---
Purpose: Detect unqualified absolutes, not marketing enthusiasm.

Detection Question:
Does the statement use absolute or superlative terms (e.g., best, unmatched, premier, world-class, only) that assert superiority without qualification, limitation, or context?

Key Guardrail:
If the absolute is clearly subjective (‚Äúwe believe we offer best-in-class service‚Äù), sub-bucket 4 does not apply unless framed as fact.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "The best investment solution available."
‚Ä¢ "Unmatched expertise in active management."
‚Ä¢ "A perfect solution for today's markets."
‚Ä¢ "The only strategy investors need."

--- Prompt Set B: "Best-in-Class" or Leadership Claims Without Definition ---
Purpose: Capture superiority claims lacking a defined universe or criteria.

Detection Question:
Does the statement assert "best-in-class," "leading," or similar status without defining the comparison group, timeframe, or evaluation criteria? Consider whether the claim could be objectively tested but is undefined.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "A best-in-class approach to portfolio construction."
‚Ä¢ "A leading provider of investment solutions."
‚Ä¢ "Among the top firms in the industry."
‚Ä¢ "A premier asset manager."

--- Prompt Set C: Superiority Claims Without Comparative Basis ---
Purpose: Flag "better than what?" claims.


Detection Question:
Does the content assert superiority (better, stronger, superior, more effective) without identifying what it is superior to or on what basis?

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Superior results."
‚Ä¢ "Better performance outcomes."
‚Ä¢ "Stronger risk management."
‚Ä¢ "More effective strategies."

--- Prompt Set D: Absolutes Embedded in Descriptive Language ---
Purpose: Detect hidden absolutes, not adjectives.

Detection Question:
Does descriptive or qualitative language embed implicit superiority that elevates opinion into assertion?

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "World-class performance."
‚Ä¢ "Exceptional results across market cycles."
‚Ä¢ "Elite investment capabilities."
‚Ä¢ "Best-of-breed risk controls."

--- Prompt Set E: Cumulative Overstatement Across Statements ---
Purpose: Capture superiority created by aggregation, not single words.


Detection Question:
Does the cumulative effect of multiple statements create an absolute or "best-in-class" impression even if no single statement does so alone? Consider headline + subhead + bullets together.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Headline claiming leadership plus bullets asserting superiority
‚Ä¢ Multiple unqualified "top," "leading," or "best" references
‚Ä¢ Repeated superlatives without grounding context

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, verify whether the content provides:
‚Ä¢ A defined comparison group or universe
‚Ä¢ Clear criteria or methodology
‚Ä¢ Timeframe or scope limitations
‚Ä¢ Supporting data or source
If the superiority claim is clearly defined and supported, do not classify under Sub-Bucket 4.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify statements that are:
‚Ä¢ Clearly framed as opinion or marketing puffery (e.g., "we believe," "in our view"), and
‚Ä¢ Not presented as objectively verifiable superiority
Opinion becomes in-scope when framed as fact or dominance.

Do NOT flag under Sub-Bucket 4:
‚Ä¢ "We believe our approach is differentiated"
‚Ä¢ "Designed to deliver high-quality solutions"
‚Ä¢ "Focused on excellence in execution"
‚Ä¢ "Committed to strong risk management"
Unless the language asserts objective superiority or dominance.

Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If absolute, superlative, or "best-in-class" language asserts superiority without appropriate qualification, definition, or evidentiary basis, classify under Sub-Bucket 4. Disclosures that do not meaningfully qualify or define the claim do not cure the overstatement.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):
‚Ä¢ Unsubstantiated factual claims ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 1
‚Ä¢ Promissory or guaranteed outcomes ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 2
‚Ä¢ Implied certainty through framing or tone ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 3
‚Ä¢ Overstated or absolute superiority claims ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 4

==========================
SUB-BUCKET 5: Unbalanced Presentation of Benefits
==========================
(Without corresponding risks, limitations, or conditions where balance is necessary to avoid misleading impressions)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements that emphasize benefits, advantages, or favorable outcomes without proportionate disclosure of material risks, limitations, assumptions, or conditions, such that the overall presentation could create a misleading impression.

--- Prompt Set A: Benefits Presented Without Nearby Risk Context ---
Purpose: Detect benefit-focused statements lacking proximate balancing language.

Detection Question:
Does the content highlight benefits or favorable characteristics without corresponding disclosure of material risks or limitations in the same section or immediate context?

Key Guardrail:
Risks disclosed elsewhere may cure Sub-Bucket 5 only if clearly linked and reasonably proximate.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "A very beneficial strategy" with no nearby risk discussion
‚Ä¢ "Enhanced return potential" without volatility or downside context
‚Ä¢ "Reduced risk profile" without explaining how risk is measured or limited

--- Prompt Set B: Asymmetric Emphasis on Upside vs Downside ---
Purpose: Capture imbalance created by disproportionate emphasis.

Detection Question:
Would a reasonable investor notice the upside more than the downside?

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Multiple benefit bullets with a single buried risk footnote
‚Ä¢ Charts highlighting gains without corresponding drawdowns
‚Ä¢ Testimonials focused only on positive outcomes

--- Prompt Set C: Risk-Reduction Claims Without Conditions or Tradeoffs ---
Purpose: Identify claims suggesting risk mitigation without acknowledging limits.

Detection Question:
Does the statement claim to reduce, manage, or limit risk without describing the conditions, assumptions, or tradeoffs under which that benefit applies? Consider whether risk reduction is framed as universal.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Reduces downside risk" without explaining scenarios where it may not
‚Ä¢ "Provides protection in volatile markets" without limits
‚Ä¢ "Lower risk approach" without defining risk metrics

Detailed Examples to Flag:

Example 1 ‚Äî Upside Emphasized, Risk Acknowledged Only Abstractly:
"The strategy offers enhanced return potential through active security selection and dynamic portfolio construction."

Example 2 ‚Äî Risk Reduction Without Tradeoffs:
"Our disciplined investment process reduces portfolio risk while pursuing long-term growth."

Example 3 ‚Äî Diversification Benefits Without Limits:
"The portfolio provides diversification benefits that can help smooth returns over time."

Example 4 ‚Äî Volatility Framed as Purely Positive:
"By managing volatility, the strategy helps create a more stable investment experience for clients."

Example 5 ‚Äî Upside-Heavy Bullet List, Minimal Risk Context:
"Key Benefits:
‚Ä¢ Access to growth opportunities
‚Ä¢ Active risk management
‚Ä¢ Flexible portfolio construction
‚Ä¢ Long-term return potential"
(Risk disclosure appears on last page in small font)

--- Prompt Set D: Benefits Stated as General Truths Without Context ---
Purpose: Capture benefit statements presented as broadly applicable facts.

Detection Question:
Does the content present benefits as broadly applicable without clarifying applicability, suitability, or circumstances? Consider whether conditions are necessary to prevent overgeneralization.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Ideal for long-term investors" without defining investor type
‚Ä¢ "Suitable for uncertain markets" without constraints
‚Ä¢ "Well-positioned for growth" without explaining drivers or risks

--- Prompt Set E: Structural or Visual Imbalance ---
Purpose: Detect imbalance created by layout, formatting, or placement.

Detection Question:
Does the structure or visual presentation emphasize benefits while diminishing the visibility or salience of risks or limitations? Consider font size, placement, headings, and callouts.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Benefits in headlines with risks only in fine print
‚Ä¢ Callout boxes highlighting upside with no balancing callout
‚Ä¢ Risk disclosures placed far from the associated benefit claim

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, confirm whether balancing language addressing risks, limitations, assumptions, or conditions:
‚Ä¢ Appears in the same section or immediate context, and
‚Ä¢ Is sufficiently clear and prominent to avoid a misleading impression
If adequate balance is present, do not classify under Sub-Bucket 5.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify content solely because:
‚Ä¢ It highlights benefits, or
‚Ä¢ Risks are disclosed elsewhere when proximity and prominence are sufficient
Balance is contextual ‚Äî not every benefit requires exhaustive risk disclosure.

Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If benefits are emphasized without proportionate, proximate disclosure of material risks, limitations, or conditions, such that a reasonable investor could form an overly favorable or incomplete understanding, classify under Sub-Bucket 5. Risk disclosures that are technically present but structurally or contextually overwhelmed do not cure imbalance.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):
‚Ä¢ Unsubstantiated facts ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 1
‚Ä¢ Promissory or guaranteed outcomes ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 2
‚Ä¢ Implied certainty through framing or tone ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 3
‚Ä¢ Overstated or absolute superiority claims ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 4
‚Ä¢ Benefit-risk imbalance ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 5

==========================
SUB-BUCKET 6: Exaggerated or Amplified Claims
==========================
(Materially overstating capability, experience, or outcomes‚Äîincluding cumulative exaggeration across statements)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements or combinations of statements that exaggerate or amplify capabilities, experience, or expected outcomes such that the overall impression materially overstates what is accurate, supportable, or reasonable ‚Äî including where exaggeration emerges cumulatively rather than from a single claim.

--- Prompt Set A: Progressive Amplification Across Edits or Versions ---
Purpose: Detect certainty or strength added through incremental wording changes.

Detection Question:
Has language been edited or escalated over time in a way that materially increases the strength, certainty, or scope of a claim? Consider whether hedged or qualified language has been replaced with stronger assertions.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "May be welcomed" edited to "is a very beneficial strategy"
‚Ä¢ "Can be used" changed to "will be used"
‚Ä¢ "Will likely play a role" revised to "will play a role"
‚Ä¢ Removal of "may," "might," or "could" from outcome statements

--- Prompt Set B: Capability or Experience Overstatement ---
Purpose: Capture exaggerated representations of expertise, scale, or track record.

Detection Question:
Does the content overstate the firm's capabilities, experience, resources, or history relative to what can be reasonably supported? Consider whether scope, scale, or maturity is implied beyond reality.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Implying institutional-scale experience where it is limited
‚Ä¢ Presenting pilot programs as fully mature solutions
‚Ä¢ Suggesting long-standing expertise for newly launched offerings
‚Ä¢ Describing narrow experience as broad or comprehensive

--- Prompt Set C: Outcome Amplification Without New Support ---
Purpose: Identify escalation of outcome claims without additional evidence.

Detection Question:
Does the content amplify expected outcomes without introducing new data, assumptions, or substantiation to justify the stronger claim? Consider whether stronger conclusions are drawn from the same facts.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Strong results" later framed as "exceptional results"
‚Ä¢ "Positive performance" escalated to "market-leading performance"
‚Ä¢ "Early success" reframed as "proven success"

--- Prompt Set D: Cumulative Effect Across Statements or Sections ---
Purpose: Capture exaggeration created by aggregation.

Detection Question:
Does the combined effect of multiple statements materially overstate capability or outcomes, even if each statement individually appears reasonable? Consider headlines, summaries, bullets, and supporting text together.

Key Guardrail:
Aggregation must change the overall impression, not just add emphasis.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Headline asserts leadership, body text asserts superiority, conclusion implies inevitability
‚Ä¢ Multiple moderate claims stacking into an exaggerated impression
‚Ä¢ Repeated positive framing without proportional context

--- Prompt Set E: Removal of Constraints, Conditions, or Scope ---
Purpose: Detect exaggeration through loss of limiting context.

Detection Question:
Have qualifiers, limitations, assumptions, or scope constraints been removed or minimized in a way that materially broadens the claim? Consider whether the claim now applies more broadly than justified.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Removing time-bound qualifiers ("to date," "initially")
‚Ä¢ Eliminating scope limits ("in select cases," "under certain conditions")
‚Ä¢ Broadening audience applicability without support
‚Ä¢ Example 1 ‚Äî Version Drift (Qualifier Removed):

‚Ä¢ Example 2 ‚Äî Moderate Claim ‚Üí Exceptional Outcome:
"The strategy has delivered strong results, positioning it as an exceptional solution for today's markets."

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, confirm whether:
‚Ä¢ The amplified claim is supported by new or additional evidence, or
‚Ä¢ The cumulative presentation materially changes the reasonable takeaway
If amplification does not materially alter the impression, do not classify under Sub-Bucket 6.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify statements solely because:
‚Ä¢ Language is polished or marketing-oriented, or
‚Ä¢ Isolated statements are slightly stronger but still supported
This sub-bucket applies only where exaggeration is material, not stylistic.

Do NOT flag under Sub-Bucket 6:
‚Ä¢ "The strategy has evolved over time"
‚Ä¢ "We continue to refine our process"
‚Ä¢ "Early feedback has been positive"
Unless the language materially overstates maturity, scope, or outcomes.

Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If claims about capability, experience, or outcomes are materially exaggerated or amplified ‚Äî whether within a single statement or cumulatively across statements ‚Äî such that a reasonable investor could overestimate what is accurate or supportable, classify under Sub-Bucket 6. Exaggeration that emerges through aggregation, escalation, or qualifier removal is in scope, even if no single statement is independently false.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):
‚Ä¢ Missing evidence ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 1
‚Ä¢ Promissory certainty ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 2
‚Ä¢ Implied certainty via framing ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 3

SUB-BUCKET 7: Vague, Ambiguous, or Undefined Claims
==========================
(Preventing reasonable investor understanding, e.g., unclear terms, undefined metrics, or amorphous descriptors)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements that rely on vague, ambiguous, undefined, or amorphous terms such that a reasonable investor cannot understand the scope, meaning, basis, or implications of the claim.

--- Prompt Set A: Undefined Qualitative Descriptors ---
Purpose: Detect descriptive language lacking clear meaning or boundaries.

Detection Question:
Does the content use qualitative descriptors without defining what they mean in practice? Consider whether the reader can reasonably interpret the claim.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Strong performance" with no explanation
‚Ä¢ "Robust risk management" without definition
‚Ä¢ "High-quality investments" without criteria
‚Ä¢ "Innovative approach" without description

--- Prompt Set B: Undefined Metrics or Measurement Terms ---
Purpose: Capture claims that reference measurement without specifying how it is measured.

Detection Question:
Does the statement reference metrics, outcomes, or evaluation standards without identifying how they are calculated, measured, or assessed? Consider whether the metric could vary materially depending on interpretation.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Attractive risk-adjusted returns" (no metric identified)
‚Ä¢ "Low volatility strategy" (no volatility measure specified)
‚Ä¢ "Superior returns" (no benchmark or timeframe)
‚Ä¢ "Optimized portfolio construction" (no optimization basis)

--- Prompt Set C: Ambiguous Comparators or Peer References ---
Purpose: Identify comparisons that lack a defined universe or reference group.

Key Test: If the content makes a comparative claim, can you identify WHAT it is being compared to? If not, flag it.

Detection Question:
Does the content compare performance, quality, or positioning without defining the comparator or peer group? Consider whether "relative to what" is unanswered.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Outperforms peers" (peer group undefined)
‚Ä¢ "Top performer" (top of what, when, and measured how)
‚Ä¢ "Leading in the industry" (industry not specified)
‚Ä¢ "Above-average results" (average of what population)

--- Prompt Set D: Unclear Scope, Timing, or Applicability ---
Purpose: Detect claims missing essential contextual boundaries.

Detection Question:
Does the statement omit key information about timing, scope, conditions, or applicability necessary to understand the claim? Consider who the claim applies to, when, and under what circumstances.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Delivers consistent results" (over what period)
‚Ä¢ "Effective in volatile markets" (which markets, when)
‚Ä¢ "Suitable for long-term investors" (definition of long-term)
‚Ä¢ "Well-positioned for growth" (growth drivers undefined)

--- Prompt Set E: Amorphous or Marketing-Oriented Language Without Substance ---
Purpose: Capture language that sounds meaningful but conveys little information.

Detection Question:
Does the statement rely on aspirational or marketing-oriented language that sounds substantive but conveys no concrete information, where that lack of clarity affects understanding of a material claim?

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Best-of-breed capabilities"
‚Ä¢ "Next-generation investment solutions"
‚Ä¢ "Holistic portfolio approach"
‚Ä¢ "Institutional-quality outcomes"

DO NOT FLAG (Important Contrast):
‚Ä¢ "Providing stability and leadership through changing market environments." (Values/Positioning)
‚Ä¢ "We bring deep, broad perspectives to global fixed income investing." (High-Level Philosophy)
‚Ä¢ "Focused on delivering long-term value for clients." (Aspirational Intent)

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, confirm whether:
‚Ä¢ Definitions, explanations, or metrics are provided elsewhere in the same section, or
‚Ä¢ The term is commonly understood and used consistently within the document
If sufficient clarification is present, do not classify under Sub-Bucket 7.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify statements solely because:
‚Ä¢ They use high-level language in introductory or branding sections, or
‚Ä¢ Detail is provided elsewhere and clearly linked to the claim
Vagueness must be material to investor understanding, not merely stylistic.

Do not flag a statement just because it sounds descriptive or high-level if:
‚Ä¢ The statement is explained by nearby facts that show what it actually means.
‚Ä¢ The language is descriptive, not comparative (not claiming to be better than others or promising specific results).
‚Ä¢ Descriptive words are backed up by adjacent facts, acting as a summary of real information ‚Äî not a vague claim on its own.


Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If a claim relies on vague, ambiguous, or undefined terms such that a reasonable investor cannot understand its meaning, scope, or basis, classify under Sub-Bucket 7. Lack of clarity that impairs understanding constitutes a misleading impression even absent falsity or exaggeration.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):
‚Ä¢ Missing evidence ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 1
‚Ä¢ Promissory certainty ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 2
‚Ä¢ Implied certainty via framing ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 3
‚Ä¢ Absolute / best-in-class claims ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 4
‚Ä¢ Benefit-risk imbalance ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 5
‚Ä¢ Material exaggeration / amplification ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 6
‚Ä¢ Vagueness or ambiguity impairing understanding ‚Üí Sub-Bucket 7

SUB-BUCKET 8: Audience-Inappropriate Language or Complexity
==========================
(Creating a misleading impression, e.g., institutional framing for retail audiences, or simplified claims masking risk)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify statements where the level of sophistication, terminology, framing, or implied assumptions exceed or mismatch the intended audience‚Äôs reasonable understanding, thereby obscuring risk, overstating accessibility, or minimizing complexity.

--- Prompt Set A: Institutional or Technical Framing for Retail Audiences ---
Purpose: Detect sophisticated or insider language presented without necessary explanation.

Detection Question:
Does the content use institutional, technical, or professional terminology without explanation when directed to a retail or general audience? Consider whether the audience can reasonably understand the implications.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ References to "risk-adjusted alpha," "factor tilts," or "basis points" without explanation
‚Ä¢ Complex portfolio construction concepts presented without plain-language context
‚Ä¢ Institutional assumptions (e.g., liquidity, sophistication, scale) applied to retail readers

--- Prompt Set B: Oversimplification That Masks Risk or Complexity ---
Purpose: Capture simplification that distorts understanding.

Detection Question:
Does the content oversimplify strategies, risks, or outcomes in a way that minimizes complexity or masks material risk for the intended audience? Consider whether simplification replaces explanation.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "A simple way to grow your money" without risk context
‚Ä¢ "Designed to smooth out market ups and downs" without explaining limits
‚Ä¢ "Hands-off investing made easy" without describing tradeoffs

--- Prompt Set C: Mismatch Between Product Complexity and Audience Expectations ---
Purpose: Identify audience-product mismatches.

Detection Question:
Is complex or nuanced information presented as broadly suitable or easily understood without clarifying prerequisites, suitability, or investor sophistication? Consider whether the audience is implicitly assumed to have expertise.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Complex strategies framed as universally appropriate
‚Ä¢ Advanced investment structures described without prerequisites
‚Ä¢ Nuanced risks summarized as simple benefits

--- Prompt Set D: Marketing Tone That Encourages Overconfidence ---
Purpose: Detect tone that may lead the audience to underestimate risk.

Detection Question:
Does the tone or presentation encourage confidence or certainty beyond what the audience should reasonably infer? Consider whether tone substitutes for explanation.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Invest with confidence" without accompanying risk discussion
‚Ä¢ "A smarter way to invest" without explaining tradeoffs
‚Ä¢ "Professional-grade results made accessible" without clarifying differences

--- Prompt Set E: Inconsistent Audience Signals Within the Same Content ---
Purpose: Capture confusion created by mixed audience targeting.

Detection Question:
Does the content switch between institutional-level detail and retail-level simplification in a way that could confuse or mislead the audience? Consider whether audience assumptions are inconsistent.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Retail marketing language paired with institutional performance framing
‚Ä¢ Simplified headlines with highly technical footnotes
‚Ä¢ Mixed signals about required knowledge or experience

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, confirm:
‚Ä¢ The intended audience of the content (e.g., retail, institutional, intermediary), and
‚Ä¢ Whether explanations, definitions, or context are provided that appropriately bridge complexity
If language is appropriately tailored and explained for the audience, do not classify under Sub-Bucket 8.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify content solely because:
‚Ä¢ It uses technical language for an institutional or professional audience, or
‚Ä¢ It simplifies concepts appropriately while still conveying material risks and limitations
Audience-appropriate simplification is permitted; misleading simplification is not.

Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If language, framing, or complexity is misaligned with the intended audience such that a reasonable investor could misunderstand risks, requirements, or implications, classify under Sub-Bucket 8. Misleading impressions may arise from how information is presented, not just what is stated.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):

SUB-BUCKET 9: Unfair, Deceptive, or Unclear Communications
==========================
(That could reasonably result in consumer or investor harm‚Äîincluding misleading omissions or confusing structuring)

Core Detection Prompt (Primary):
Identify communications that, taken as a whole, are unfair, deceptive, or unclear in a manner that could reasonably cause consumer or investor harm ‚Äî including through misleading omissions, confusing structure, or presentation that obscures material information.

--- Prompt Set A: Misleading Omissions of Material Information ---
Purpose: Detect harm arising from what is left out.

Detection Question:
Does the communication omit material information necessary to prevent a misleading impression, given what is affirmatively stated? Consider whether the omission alters the reasonable takeaway.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Discussing benefits or features without disclosing material limitations
‚Ä¢ Describing a strategy without noting key risks or constraints
‚Ä¢ Omitting conditions that materially affect applicability or outcomes

--- Prompt Set B: Confusing or Obscuring Structure ---
Purpose: Capture harm created by how information is organized.

Detection Question:
Is the communication structured in a way that obscures, dilutes, or distracts from material information? Consider placement, sequencing, and emphasis.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Important caveats buried after lengthy promotional content
‚Ä¢ Risk disclosures separated from related claims by multiple sections
‚Ä¢ Dense blocks of text that make material information difficult to identify

--- Prompt Set C: Mixed or Contradictory Messaging ---
Purpose: Identify confusion caused by inconsistency.

Detection Question:
Does the content contain mixed, contradictory, or internally inconsistent messages that could confuse a reasonable investor? Consider whether the reader is left with unclear or conflicting impressions.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Statements minimizing risk alongside warnings implying significant risk
‚Ä¢ Claims suggesting broad applicability paired with narrow disclaimers
‚Ä¢ Conflicting descriptions of strategy objectives or constraints

--- Prompt Set D: Language or Framing That Exploits Cognitive Biases ---
Purpose: Detect unfair persuasion tactics that may lead to harm.

Detection Question:
Does the language or framing exploit urgency, fear, authority, or confidence in a way that could impair reasonable judgment? Consider whether persuasion overrides understanding.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ "Now is the time" messaging without context
‚Ä¢ Appeals to authority that discourage independent evaluation
‚Ä¢ Framing that pressures decisions without balanced information

--- Prompt Set E: Reasonable Likelihood of Investor or Consumer Harm ---
Purpose: Anchor classification to harm, not technicality.

Detection Question:
Could the communication, as presented, reasonably lead an investor or consumer to make a decision they would not have made with clear, complete, and balanced information? Consider financial, suitability, or expectation harm.

Examples to flag:
‚Ä¢ Overstated confidence leading to inappropriate risk-taking
‚Ä¢ Confusion about product features or limitations
‚Ä¢ Misunderstanding of costs, risks, or obligations

Verification Instruction (Critical Guardrail):
Before classifying under this sub-bucket, confirm:
‚Ä¢ The issue is not more precisely addressed by Sub-Buckets 1‚Äì8, and
‚Ä¢ The communication, viewed holistically, presents a reasonable risk of misunderstanding or harm
This sub-bucket is intended for residual risk, not first-line classification.

Non-Trigger Clarifier (Guardrail):
Do not classify communications solely because:
‚Ä¢ They are persuasive or marketing-oriented, or
‚Ä¢ A reasonable investor would still clearly understand the material terms and risks
Harm must be reasonably foreseeable, not speculative.

Reviewer/Agent Resolution Instruction:
If a communication is unfair, deceptive, or unclear in its overall presentation, such that a reasonable consumer or investor could be misled or harmed ‚Äî including through omissions, confusing structure, or cumulative effects ‚Äî classify under Sub-Bucket 9. This classification may apply even where no single statement is independently false or promissory.

Boundary Reminder (Theme 1 Alignment):
